Introduction

Globally, 1 in 6 people die from cancer. Despite their differences, all cancers share a common hallmark: uncontrolled cell proliferation.

In healthy cells, division occurs through mitosis, producing two new cells in a tightly regulated process known as the cell cycle. In cancer cells, however, the cell cycle becomes abnormally fast and uncontrolled, leading to tumor formation.

Shutting Down the Cell Cycle

The cell cycle consists of four distinct phases, each governed by specific enzymes, primarily the cyclin-dependent kinases (CDKs).

For instance, CDK2, when bound to cyclin B, ensures the transition from the S phase to the G2 phase of the cycle.

Research Question

Could we find inhibitors that target the CDKs/cyclin complex and reduce their catalytic activity using BindingDB?

Study of CDKs

We analyzed 47 CDKs and their amino-acid sequences. The key questions were:

  • Are the CDKs different enough for unique ligand interactions?
  • Or do similarities allow us to classify them into families?

Study of Ligands

We examined ligands bound to CDKs to determine structural patterns among those with high affinity.

Key findings:

  • Strong-affinity ligands within the same CDK family share ~25% molecular similarity.
  • Molecular conformation plays a significant role in ligand binding.
